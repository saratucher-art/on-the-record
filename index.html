Section 01 — The Problem

When the Camera
Goes Off, Justice Falters

In courtrooms and interrogation rooms across the country, the outcome of a case often rests not on objective facts, but on whose account is believed. Without continuous, reliable recordings, critical gaps emerge — and in those gaps, truth can become a matter of interpretation.

Section 02 — Why It Matters

This Affects Everyone
— Not Just the Accused

This isn't a partisan issue. Mandatory recording protects individuals from coercive or abusive interrogation, while simultaneously protecting officers from false accusations. It's a structural safeguard for the whole system.

⚖️

Fairness & Truth

Legal outcomes should be based on what actually happened, not on whose word is more convincing. A complete recording provides an objective, unbiased account that no testimony can match.

🛡️

Mutual Protection

Officers accused falsely deserve the same protection as citizens who are coerced. Full recordings clear the innocent on both sides of the interrogation table — without ambiguity.

🔍

Accountability

When those in authority know interactions are recorded, behavior changes. Studies show misconduct drops and professional conduct improves significantly in departments with recording requirements.

🧠

Memory Is Fallible

Human memory is reconstructive, not photographic. Research shows even trained professionals misremember details under stress — a fact courts cannot ignore when recordings aren't available.

🏛️

Public Trust

Communities with transparent police departments report higher rates of cooperation and trust. Transparency in procedure builds the institutional legitimacy the justice system depends on.

💰

Cost of Injustice

Wrongful conviction cases cost municipalities millions in settlements. Preventing even a handful through mandatory recording more than offsets the cost of camera systems and storage infrastructure.

Section 03 — The Reality

What Happens When
Recording Stops

False Confessions Are More Common Than You Think

It seems impossible: why would someone confess to a crime they didn't commit? But psychological research reveals that high-stress interrogation environments, sleep deprivation, isolation, and leading questions can push innocent people into confessions — especially young people and those with cognitive vulnerabilities.

"People confess to crimes they didn't commit more often than any of us would like to believe." — Saul Kassin, psychologist and leading researcher on false confessions

The Reid Technique, used widely in U.S. interrogations for decades, has been specifically criticized for its tendency to produce false confessions through psychological pressure. Courts often treat a confession as near-conclusive evidence — making what happens before it absolutely critical to document.

Without recording, there is no way to assess whether a confession was voluntary. A full recording doesn't just capture the words — it captures the conditions. Learn more at the Innocence Project →

Body Cameras Alone Are Not Enough

Body-worn cameras have become a centerpiece of police reform conversations since 2014. But without clear policies requiring activation, continuous recording, and preserved footage, they are often more symbol than solution.

⚠️ In a 2021 analysis, the Brennan Center found that in several major cities, officers failed to activate body cameras in over 20% of incidents where policy required it — with inconsistent disciplinary consequences.

Body cameras face technical issues too: batteries die, memory fills up, officers forget to activate them, and footage sometimes "can't be located" after high-profile incidents. Policy must mandate not just cameras, but continuous, uninterrupted recording with clear chain-of-custody requirements for footage storage.

Brennan Center for Justice: Body Camera Research →

The Legal Landscape Is Uneven — and Insufficient

As of 2024, only 29 U.S. states have any statewide requirement for recording custodial interrogations in felony cases. The remaining states leave policy to individual departments — which can mean anything from thorough recording protocols to no requirements at all.

Even in states with laws, loopholes are common: equipment failure exceptions, partial-recording allowances, and limited crime category requirements mean the protection is often less than it appears on paper.

Illinois was the first state to mandate electronic recording of custodial interrogations in homicide cases in 2003. Studies showed it reduced false confessions and improved jury confidence in legitimate confessions alike.

Federal law still does not require recording of interrogations. The FBI only voluntarily adopted recording practices as recently as 2014 — and only for certain crimes. National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers: Recording Tracker →

Research Consistently Supports Mandatory Recording

Academic studies and policy analyses spanning decades point in one direction: mandatory recording of interrogations improves outcomes for everyone involved — defendants, officers, prosecutors, and the public.

A 2004 study by Thomas Sullivan found that after Illinois adopted mandatory recording laws, prosecutors reported that recordings helped convict guilty defendants more often than they helped the defense — because legitimate confessions became unimpeachable.

Studies also show that when officers know they are being recorded, they use better interview techniques, are less likely to engage in coercive tactics, and produce confessions that are both more reliable and more legally durable.

The reform isn't about distrust of law enforcement. It's about creating a system where everyone benefits from clarity.

Section 04 — The Solution

What Mandatory Recording
Actually Looks Like

The policy framework isn't complicated. Clear, enforceable standards already exist in model legislation. What's missing is the political will to adopt them universally.

01

Full Custodial Interrogation Recording

Any interrogation conducted after arrest must be recorded in its entirety — from the moment the individual enters the room to the moment they leave. No gaps. No "technical difficulties" exceptions without documentation and review.

02

Mandatory Body Camera Activation Policies

Officers must activate body cameras at the start of every law enforcement encounter with the public. Footage must be preserved for a defined period with strict chain-of-custody rules. Failure to activate should carry clear, documented consequences.

03

Suppression Consequences for Non-Compliance

Courts should apply a rebuttable presumption against the prosecution when recordings are missing — meaning if law required recording and it wasn't done, the burden shifts. This creates real incentive for compliance beyond policy language. See model legislation →

04

Federal Baseline Standard

State variation creates unequal justice depending on zip code. A federal floor requirement for all custodial interrogations in serious crimes would create consistent protections nationwide while allowing states to exceed the minimum. Support the Innocence Project's work →

Where States Stand on Recording Laws

  • Illinois — Full mandatory recording since 2003 (expanded 2014)
  • California — SB 923 expanded recording requirements statewide
  • New York — Mandatory for homicides and Class A felonies

About This Project

Our Mission

"On The Record is an advocacy initiative committed to the simple belief that the justice system must document its own process. Mandatory, uninterrupted recording of police interrogations and encounters is not a radical demand — it is the minimum standard a fair system owes to every citizen and every officer. We believe transparency protects everyone."

This project was created as a civic advocacy initiative exploring the gap between current recording policy and the standards that a truly transparent justice system demands. It is intended to inform and encourage civic engagement, not to advocate for or against any political party or specific legislation.

What We Stand For

  • ⚖️
    Fairness Legal outcomes should be based on complete, accurate information — not the luck of who tells a more compelling story under pressure.
  • 🔍
    Accountability Authority requires transparency. Recording is not surveillance of the innocent — it's documentation of power in action.
  • 🛡️
    Protection for All This issue transcends politics. It protects suspects from coercion and protects officers from false claims — equally and simultaneously.
  • 🗣️
    Civic Engagement You don't need a law degree to demand better from the systems that govern your life. Informed citizens are the engine of policy change.
